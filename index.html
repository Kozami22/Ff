First of all let's define what is the F1

Formula 1 (F1) is the highest class of international racing for open-wheel single-seater formula racing cars.
It is sanctioned by the Fédération Internationale de l'Automobile (FIA) and is widely considered the pinnacle of motorsport.
​Here is a breakdown of what makes F1 unique:

Core Concepts

  • The "Formula": The name refers to a strict set of rules and regulations that all participants' cars must conform to. These rules govern everything from engine displacement to aerodynamic dimensions.
  • The Cars: F1 cars are the fastest regulated road-course racing cars in the world, capable of reaching speeds over 370 km/h and pulling up to 5G or 6G in corners.
  • The Season: A season consists of a series of races known as Grand Prix, held on purpose-built circuits and sometimes on closed city streets (like Monaco or Las Vegas).

The Competition

F1 is unique because it is both a team sport and an individual sport. At the end of every season, two world championships are awarded:

Technical Innovation

F1 is often described as a "laboratory on wheels." Many technologies we see in everyday road cars—such as active suspension, carbon fiber construction, and advanced hybrid energy recovery systems—were originally developed or refined in Formula 1.
​Note: Modern F1 cars use 1.6-liter V6 turbocharged hybrid power units, which are some of the most thermally efficient engines ever created.
